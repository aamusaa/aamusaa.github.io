Grip


Project objective

The objective of the GRIP mobile app is to serve as a digital guidebook for outdoor climbers. This app concept came to me 3 years ago when I wanted to improve my design skills with a personal UX/UI project (Grippii). Back then, I designed the app to act as a social network for rock climbing enthusiasts. At the end of the project, I realized that from a practical standpoint, the app was trying to do too much. I didn’t want to design a climbing experience that revolved around a digital interface (i.e. researching, making plans, chatting, posting photos). Thus, I discerned the most value of the app concept came from the guide portion as it would eliminate the need for physical books in different languages with different grading systems.


Research

I began my design process by examining existing climbings apps. While there are many emerging apps in the marketplace for tracking climbs, I focused on mobile guidebooks to assess the sort of features and functions I’ve been exploring with Grip. When I first began the design process for this app concept, there were only a couple of competitors in the app store with dense, awkward UIs. A few years later, there are a handful of solutions with far better UI design and functionality. Nevertheless, there are components I personally could see improvement on and have decided to incorporate within Grip. I included some of my critique and observations for a few climbing apps below.

*When I use the word “topos” in this project, I am referring to a graphical representation of a climbing route



User Flow

I came away from my research with the challenge of incorporating the important details found in physical guidebooks within a clear format for a small field of real estate (mobile screen). I made a flowchart to organize my thoughts on how a user would navigate through different features of the app. At this point in the app concept, a user would only be able to submit a filtered search query for climbing crags and save specific routes.



Wireframes

After fleshing out the necessary functions and page interactions conceptually, I started to design some wireframes.



Branding

Before I invested more time into the visual details of the app, I decided to work out the branding so as to maintain some consistency and cohesion in the app’s design. Years ago, I named this app concept Grippii, playing on the action most used in rock climbing (gripping rock), but since then, have shortened the name to Grip.



Mockups

Employing my wireframes and branding guidelines for direction, I began to craft a new set of mockups.



User Testing

With my collection of mockup screens, I set about to user test Grip with real climbers. Below are summarized notes from a couple of my testing sessions.

Rick
M, 10 years climbing experience

  • prefers to using books over apps because apps miss certain details and have a poor layout
  • would like details such a access length, public transportation, kid-friendly, wet/dry weather climbing
  • switch from satellite to map view
  • thinks organizing route on crag from A-Z and R-L to be redundant, better to keep L-R

Lauren
F, 19 years climbing experience

  • uses books with the exception of her phone on a multi-pitch
  • doesn’t like to require reception to view mobile route details
  • needs details such as crag closures due to falcons nesting (for example)
  • likes the ability to add notes to routes such as personal grading and whether it was climbed onsite/flash/redpoint (climbing terminology)

After user testing, I made a list of the most important updates to improve Grip:

  • organize routes on crags by L-R and have topos selectable
  • side scroll crags in a given area
  • include more details of crags with climbing icons found in guidebooks

Mockups V2

I expanded my set of icons to give the user more context about the kind of climbing involved for their crag of interest.



Below is the latest set of screens I’ve created, with some new updates based on user feedback.



Next Steps

As I’ve been casually revising this app for about 3 years now, I don’t see an end point in the near future. I find it quite fun to apply new ideas and skills and insights to old work.

Already, I’m mulling over a couple questions for my next iteration:

  • Once a crag result from a filter is saved, does the saved item show the filtered specs or the general specs of that crag?
  • Is it enough to provide info about a crag or would specific route betas be a valuable addition?

I’ve already begun to prototype this app using Origami Studio, and will release a video of the screen flow once I’ve settled on suitable solutions to the aforementioned questions.


Mito


Roles

UX/UI Design, Market Research


Challenge

As part of a 4th-year design project, my team was challenged to create a provotype (provocative prototype) based on a piece of furniture provided by our Hong Kong-based client, ELMOOD.



The provotype is the first part of a series of design challenges for this client. My work for the second part, the experimental prototype, can be viewed here.

The point of this exercise was to provoke a reaction from a consumer by challenging standard product design. In a way, a provotype can be thought of as a physical embodiment of the question “What if….”.


Solution

As our client was based in Hong Kong, my group looked at current issues in that environment to inspire our brainstorming process. Environmental sustainability emerged as a common long-standing concern. My team collectively decided our design intention was to encourage Hong Kong residents to adopt sustainable lifestyle changes by highlighting their energy consumption habits.


Our provotype design utilizes the Redondo’s existing frame to house a pair of bionic lungs anchored to the ceiling of the frame’s interior. Here’s a quick mockup I made of the provotype, Mito:



Mito is outfitted with technology that connects to smart devices in a household. The provotype unit measures energy consumption from these devices and by determining the overall level of environmental sustainability, the bionic lungs will “swell” or “wither”.

The smart devices linked to the Mito are managed via mobile. Below are a few UI wireframes I designed to show some of the control features.


Another group member designed the energy usage stats screens, while my focus was on the connected devices and appliances.


As this project was more of a “hypothetical” design challenge, I did not worry too much about the feasibility of the app in the real world. However, a few months later, I consulted an electrician and updated the design to more accurately depict the main function of tracking energy consumption.

Some notable changes I implemented are:

  1. Switching the A/C tab to HVAC (to represent heating, ventilation AND air conditioning) with individual room selection
  2. Cutting the water pressure feature
  3. Cutting the provocative lung concept in lieu of a simple home page


Redondo


Roles

Product Design, Graphic Design, UX Research


Challenge

In my final year of undergrad studies, I collaborated with a team of 6 peers to redesign a piece of furniture for our Hong Kong-based client, ELMOOD. We were tasked with creating a new user experience for the Redondo mobile bookcase.


This experimental prototype is the second part of a series of design challenges for this client. My work for the first part, the provocative prototype, can be viewed here.


Solution

After conducting a demographic breakdown for ELMOOD’s client base, my team decided to focus on the Hong Kong market - specifically design-conscious consumers in their 20s-30s with a comfortable income that would enable them to purchase high-quality furniture. We dug into social issues that affected this market segment and personal stress came out as a dominant concern. Thus, we decided to modify the Redondo mobile bookcase, into a meditative workspace.

ØMO


Roles

Product Design, UX Research, 3D Modelling


Team

Kayla Trivieri, Steven Plat, Cherry Vong


Challenge

As part of a class project for the RSA Design competition, my team was challenged to create a sustainable solution for the linear economic model of fast-moving consumer goods (FMCGs). The “take, make, dispose” linear model of FMCGs strains our natural resources and produces highly disposable products that clutter our landfills, incinerators, and wastewater for hundreds, maybe thousands of years. My team chose to redesign of one of the most universally used FMCGs, the deodorant.



Advertisements for Mennen deodorant from the 1960s - Deodorant packaging has seen little innovation in the last half century.


Solution

Before drafting up prototypes, my team surveyed 200+ participants about their stance on environmental sustainability and consumption habits for FMCGs. Our UX research process then led us to conduct “fly on the wall” observations at major retailers that sell deodorants, experimental material testing (temperatures, shipping conditions), and user testing with participants of different genders and ages to refine our ever-evolving product design to meet the needs of potential consumers.

Design

After all our UX research and testing, my team settled on the final prototype form for our sustainable deodorant product, ØMO.



I animated a 3D model I built in Cinema4D to simulate the deodorant mechanics.


Deodorant Mechanic Simulation from AMINA MUSA on Vimeo.


ØMO tackles the issue of unsustainable product design by using reusable and biodegradable materials to replace standard plastic cases currently manufactured by Unilever and P&G (leading producers for solid stick deodorant). ØMO offers a durable casing made out of three components: a base, a core, and a cap. These components act as a housing unit for the replaceable inserts which can be purchased separately. The easy-to-assemble ØMO will allow users to choose a casing unit that they desire as well as the deodorant product they love. Once the insert is used up, the consumer can discard the remains of the biodegradable insert, clean the dishwasher-safe casing unit, and refill with a new insert.
